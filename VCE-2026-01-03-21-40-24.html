10. The Syllogistic Formula

Terms in a Syllogism

A standard categorical syllogism contains exactly three terms, each appearing twice across the three propositions. Identifying them is the first step in checking for validity.

The Trio of Logic

1. The Major Term (P)

The predicate of the conclusion. It represents the broader category.

Location: Major Premise & Conclusion.

2. The Minor Term (S)

The subject of the conclusion. It represents the specific case or individual.

Location: Minor Premise & Conclusion.

3. The Middle Term (M)

The "Bridge." It connects the Major and Minor terms but never appears in the conclusion.

Location: Major Premise & Minor Premise.

The Logical Architecture

Major Premise: All [Lawyers] are [Educated].

Minor Premise: [Browne] is a [Lawyer].

Conclusion: Therefore, [Browne] is [Educated].

13. The Four-Fold Scheme

Standard Form Categorical Propositions

Every categorical proposition has a specific Letter Name (A, E, I, O). This system was developed to make the logic of language as precise as mathematics.

Type A

Universal Affirmative

Form: All S is P.

It includes the entire subject class within the predicate class.

"All Judges are Lawyers."

Type E

Universal Negative

Form: No S is P.

It excludes the entire subject class from the predicate class.

"No Felons are Voters."

Type I

Particular Affirmative

Form: Some S is P.

It asserts that at least one member of S is also a member of P.

Particular Negative

It asserts that at least one member of S is excluded from P.

15. Linguistic Translation

Standard vs. Non-Standard

Logic requires a strict format. Most sentences we speak are Non-Standard. To analyze them, we must translate them into the Standard A, E, I, O forms.

Non-Standard Form

Everyday language that is often vague, missing a quantifier, or uses non-standard verbs.

  • "Lawyers are rich."
  • "Not everyone is a thief."
  • "Birds fly."

Standard Form

Must have: Quantifier + Subject + Copula + Predicate.

  • "All lawyers are rich people." (A)
  • "Some people are not thieves." (O)
  • "All birds are flying creatures." (A)

Rules of Translation

1. Identify the Quantifier: Is it 'All', 'No', or 'Some'?

2. Fix the Copula: Always use 'is', 'are', 'is not', or 'are not'.

3. Standardize the Terms: The subject and predicate must be nouns or noun phrases.

16. The Logical Dimensions

Quality & Quantity

Every proposition is defined by these two properties. Quantity tells us how many; Quality tells us what kind of action (Affirming or Denying).

1. Quantity (The Scope)

Refers to the number of members of the subject class being discussed.

  • Universal: Refers to All members (All, No, Every).
  • Particular: Refers to Some members (Some, At least one).

2. Quality (The Nature)

Refers to whether the proposition affirms or denies the predicate of the subject.

  • Affirmative: States that S is P.
  • Negative: States that S is not P.

17. Logical Relationships

The Square of Opposition

This diagram represents the Immediate Inferences you can make about the truth or falsity of one proposition based on another.

Contradictories (A & O, E & I)

They have opposite truth values. If A is true, O must be false. They cannot both be true and cannot both be false.

Contraries (A & E)

Both cannot be true, but both can be false. If "All S is P" is true, then "No S is P" must be false.

Sub-Contraries (I & O)

Both cannot be false, but both can be true. If "Some S is not P" is false, then "Some S is P" must be true.

Subalternation (A & I, E & O)

Truth flows downward (if All is true, Some is true). Falsity flows upward (if Some is false, All is false).

18. Deductive Inference

The Categorical Syllogism

A syllogism is a deductive argument consisting of exactly three categorical propositions containing exactly three terms.

The 1-2-3 Rule

1. Major Premise

Contains the Major Term (P) and Middle Term (M).

2. Minor Premise

Contains the Minor Term (S) and Middle Term (M).

3. Conclusion

Contains the Minor Term (S) and Major Term (P).

Browne's Rules of Validity

If a syllogism breaks any of these, it is Invalid.

  • 1. Exactly three terms.
  • 2. Exactly three propositions.
  • 3. Middle term must be distributed once.
  • 4. No term distributed in conclusion if not in premise.
  • 5. No conclusion from two negative premises.
  • 6. If one premise is negative, conclusion must be negative.
  • 7. No conclusion from two particular premises.
  • 8. If one premise is particular, conclusion must be particular.

20. The Logic of Alternatives

Disjunctive Syllogism

The Disjunctive Syllogism works by Elimination. It starts with a disjunction (Either P or Q) and uses a process of denial to find the truth.

The Rule of Tollendo Ponens

"The mode that affirms by denying." By denying one alternative, you automatically affirm the other.

Either P or Q.
Not P.
‚à¥ Therefore, Q.

Legal Application

"Either the defendant was at the crime scene, or he was at home. We have proven he was NOT at home."

Conclusion: He was at the crime scene.

Inclusive Disjunction

"Either P or Q (or both)." Like saying "I want cream or sugar in my coffee." You could have both!

Exclusive Disjunction

"Either P or Q (but not both)." Like a court verdict: "Guilty or Not Guilty." There is no middle ground.

The Elimination Chamber

Can you identify the valid elimination?

"The contract is either valid or void. It is not void. Therefore, it is valid."

"Either Browne is a student or he is a genius. Browne is a student. Therefore, he is not a genius." (Assume Inclusive Disjunction)

21. The Hybrid Logic

Mixed Hypothetical Syllogism

In a "Mixed" syllogism, we transition from possibility to certainty. The Minor Premise acts as the trigger that activates the conclusion.

1. Mixed Conditional

The most common type. It uses a Conditional major and a Categorical minor.

Major: If you are a Senator, you are a citizen.
Minor: Browne is a Senator.
Conclusion: Browne is a citizen.

2. Mixed Disjunctive

Uses a Disjunctive major and a Categorical minor to eliminate options.

Major: Either he is a thief or he is innocent.
Minor: He is NOT a thief.
Conclusion: He is innocent.

A dilemma is a syllogism with a Disjunctive Major Premise and two Conditional Minor Premises. It forces an opponent to choose between two options, both leading to the same (usually unpleasant) result.
