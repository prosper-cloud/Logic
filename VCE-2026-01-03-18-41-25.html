Introduction Jurisprudence Classification Sources Legal Reasoning

1. Introduction to Legal Method

Legal method is the study of the techniques, processes, and strategies used by lawyers, judges, and legal scholars to identify, interpret, and apply the law. It is the "machinery" of the legal profession.

"Law is not just a set of rules, but a method of thinking and a system of social engineering."

2. Schools of Jurisprudence

Jurisprudence is the philosophy of law. Different schools of thought offer varying perspectives on what law is and where its authority comes from.

The Natural Law School

Proponents (like Aquinas and Cicero) argue that law is derived from a higher moral order or "nature." An unjust law is not a law ($Lex\ iniusta\ non\ est\ lex$).

The Legal Positivism School

Led by John Austin and H.L.A. Hart, this school posits that law is a command from a sovereign backed by sanctions. It separates "what the law is" from "what the law ought to be."

The Historical School

Friedrich Carl von Savigny argued that law is an organic growth of a people's spirit (the Volksgeist). It evolves with the culture and traditions of a society.

The Sociological School

Roscoe Pound viewed law as a tool for "Social Engineering"—balancing competing interests in society to achieve the greatest good with the least friction.

Legal Realism

Realists (like Oliver Wendell Holmes) argue that law is what the courts do in practice, not just what is written in books. "The life of the law has not been logic; it has been experience."

3. Classification of Law

Category Description Examples
Public Law Governs relationship between individuals and the State. Constitutional Law, Criminal Law.
Private Law Governs relationships between individuals (Civil). Contract Law, Tort, Family Law.
Substantive Law The actual rules that define rights and duties. The law defining "Theft."
Procedural Law The rules by which those rights are enforced. Evidence Law, Criminal Procedure.

4. Sources of Law

Where does the law come from? In a typical Common Law jurisdiction, the sources include:

  • The Constitution: The supreme law of the land.
  • Statutes: Laws enacted by the legislature (Acts of Parliament/National Assembly).
  • Judicial Precedent: The principle of Stare Decisis (standing by decided matters).
  • Customary Law: Rules derived from long-standing traditions of a community.
  • International Law: Treaties and conventions between nations.

5. Legal Reasoning and Interpretation

Inductive vs. Deductive Reasoning

Lawyers use deductive reasoning to apply general rules to specific facts, and inductive reasoning to derive general principles from a series of specific cases (precedents).

Canons of Statutory Interpretation

  1. Literal Rule: Words are given their plain, ordinary meaning.
  2. Golden Rule: Used if the literal rule leads to an absurdity.
  3. Mischief Rule: Looks at the "mischief" the law was intended to remedy.
  4. Purposive Approach: Interpreting the law to give effect to its overall purpose.

The Compendium on the Sources of Law

I. Definition of Sources

In legal method, "Sources of Law" refers to the origins of legal rules. These are the materials from which a person (judge, lawyer, or citizen) or a legal system derives the rules that govern conduct within a society.

Formal Sources:

The legal procedures and methods by which a rule is established (e.g., the passing of a bill by the legislature).

Material Sources:

The content or the actual substance of the law (e.g., customs, religious principles, or social values).

II. The Hierarchy of Sources

In most modern constitutional democracies, the sources follow a strict hierarchy. If a lower source conflicts with a higher source, the higher source prevails.

Level Source Name Authority
1 The Constitution Supreme Law; nullifies any inconsistent law.
2 Legislation (Statutes) Acts of Parliament/Congress or Delegated Legislation.
3 Judicial Precedents Common Law/Case Law ($Stare\ Decisis$).
4 Customary Law Established practices of a people (must pass tests of validity).

III. Primary Sources of Law

Statutory Law

1. Legislation

Legislation is the deliberate enactment of legal rules by an organ of government (the Legislature). It is the most robust source of law in modern times because it can abrogate (cancel) existing common law or custom.

Case Law

2. Judicial Precedents

Also known as "Judge-made law." It operates on the principle of Stare Decisis. When a superior court makes a decision, that decision becomes binding on all lower courts within the same jurisdiction for similar future cases.

  • Ratio Decidendi: The reason for the decision (Binding).
  • Obiter Dictum: Something said "by the way" (Persuasive only).
Legacy Law

3. Received English Law (Common Law Jurisdictions)

Many jurisdictions incorporate English Common Law, the Doctrines of Equity, and Statutes of General Application as a foundational source.

IV. Secondary Sources

These sources are not binding on their own but are used to persuade the court or to clarify the meaning of primary sources.

  • Law Reports: Published volumes of judicial decisions.
  • Legal Textbooks: Works by eminent jurists and scholars.
  • Legal Periodicals/Journals: Academic reviews of legal trends.
  • Legal Dictionaries: Defining technical terminology (e.g., Black's Law Dictionary).

V. Validity Tests for Customary Law

Before a custom is accepted as a source of law by a court, it usually must pass several tests:

  1. The Mirror Test: Is it a true reflection of the current people's practice?
  2. Repugnancy Test: Is it repugnant to natural justice, equity, and good conscience?
  3. Public Policy Test: Is it contrary to public interest or existing statutes?

Foundations of Legal Philosophy: Quid est Lex?

The Complexity of Definition

Defining "Law" is notoriously difficult. As H.L.A. Hart noted, few questions concerning human society have been asked with such persistence and answered by serious thinkers in so many diverse, strange, and even paradoxical ways.

At its core, law is a system of rules created and enforced through social or governmental institutions to regulate behavior.

The Fathers of Jurisprudence

To understand what law is, we must look at the giants upon whose shoulders the legal system stands.

Thomas Aquinas The Theological Father

"Law is an ordinance of reason for the common good, made by him who has care of the community, and promulgated."

He divided law into four: Eternal Law, Natural Law, Divine Law, and Human Law.

John Austin The Father of Command Theory

"Law is the command of the sovereign, backed by the threat of a sanction."

He insisted that "The existence of law is one thing; its merit or demerit is another."

H.L.A. Hart The Analytical Giant

"Law is a union of primary and secondary rules."

He criticized Austin, arguing law is more than just "the gunman situation writ large."

Hans Kelsen The Architect of the 'Grundnorm'

"Law is a system of norms."

He developed the Hierarchy of Norms, stating all law derives its validity from a 'Basic Norm' or Grundnorm.

Von Savigny The Voice of the People

"Law is the expression of the Volksgeist (The National Spirit)."

He believed law cannot be made; it is found within the history and habits of a nation.

Oliver Wendell Holmes The Great Dissenter

"The prophecies of what the courts will do in fact, and nothing more pretentious, are what I mean by the law."

He pioneered the "Bad Man Theory"—understanding law from the perspective of someone worried about consequences.

The Characteristics of Law

Regardless of the school of thought, most legal scholars agree that Law possesses these traits:

  • Normativity: It prescribes how people *ought* to behave.
  • Coerciveness: It is backed by the organized force of the state.
  • Generality: It applies to categories of people, not just individuals.
  • Institutionalization: It is created and applied by specific organs (Legislature/Courts).
