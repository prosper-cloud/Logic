01. The Definition

What is Logic?

Logic is not just "common sense." It is the formal science of correct reasoning. In the legal world, logic ensures that a conclusion (The Verdict) is the unavoidable result of the facts (The Evidence).

Without logic, law is merely an exercise of power. With logic, law becomes a pursuit of Truth.

02. The Two Paths

Reasoning Methods

Deductive Reasoning

Moving from a general rule to a specific case. If the rule is true and the facts fit, the result is Certain.

Example: All citizens have rights. You are a citizen. Therefore, you have rights.

Inductive Reasoning

Moving from specific observations to a general conclusion. This deals with Probability, not certainty.

Example: Every contract signed with this firm has been valid. Therefore, the next one likely will be too.

03. The Legal Engine

The Syllogism

This is the 3-step heart of a legal argument:

  1. Major Premise: The Law (e.g., "Theft is a crime").
  2. Minor Premise: The Fact (e.g., "John took the car").
  3. Conclusion: The Verdict (e.g., "John committed a crime").

If the first two are solid, the third is unshakeable.

04. The Medium of Thought

Language: The Key to Logic

Logic and Language are inseparable. While Logic provides the internal skeleton of a thought, Language provides the flesh and blood that makes it visible to the world. For the legal mind, words are not just symbols; they are precise tools of definition.

Function 01

Informative

Used to describe the world or report facts. This is the primary language of evidence and testimony.

Function 02

Expressive

Used to vent feelings or evoke emotions. Important in jury appeals, but must be controlled by logic.

Function 03

Directive

Used to cause or prevent action. This is the language of Statutes and Laws (e.g., "Thou shalt not...").

Why Language matters in Logic:

  • 1. Ambiguity vs. Vagueness: Logic helps a lawyer identify when a word has two meanings (ambiguity) or no clear boundary (vagueness).
  • 2. Definition: In Law, he who defines the terms wins the argument. Logic provides the rules for lexical and stipulative definitions.
  • 3. The Proposition: A sentence is just words; a proposition is the logical meaning behind it. Logic studies the truth of the proposition.

06. Formal Metalogic

The Three Virtues of a System

In 100lvl Logic, we evaluate not just single arguments, but entire logical systems (like the Nigerian Constitution or Criminal Code) based on these three properties:

Consistency

A system is consistent if it contains no contradictions. In a consistent legal system, it is impossible for a law to say "Action X is legal" and "Action X is illegal" at the same time.

Goal: Avoidance of Contradiction.

Soundness

Soundness ensures that the rules of the system only lead to true conclusions. If you follow the logical steps correctly, you are guaranteed to never reach a false result.

Goal: Factual Reliability.

Completeness

A system is complete if every true statement within it can be proven using the rules of that system. It means there are no "gaps" or "loopholes" in the logical fabric.

Goal: Total Coverage.

"A perfect legal code is Consistent (never argues with itself), Sound (always produces justice), and Complete (covers every possible crime)."

07. The Axioms

The Three Laws of Thought

These laws are self-evident. You cannot prove them; you must accept them before you can even begin to argue a case in court.

A = A

1. Law of Identity

Everything is what it is. A thing is identical with itself. In law, if a witness says a "Blue Car" was at the scene, that car cannot suddenly be a "Red Truck" in the same context.

Formula: "A is A"

2. Law of Non-Contradiction

A statement cannot be both true and false at the same time and in the same sense. A defendant cannot be "Guilty" and "Not Guilty" of the exact same act simultaneously.

Formula: "A cannot be B and not-B"

3. Law of Excluded Middle

For any proposition, it is either true or its negation is true. There is no middle ground. You either committed the crime or you did not; logic does not allow a third "maybe" state.

Formula: "Either A or not-A"

08. The Core of Advocacy

The Anatomy of Argument

What is an Argument?

In logic, an argument is not a "quarrel." it is a group of propositions where one (the Conclusion) is claimed to follow from the others (the Premises). Without this relationship, you just have a list of sentences.

Premise(s)

The evidence, facts, or reasons provided for support.

Inference

The logical move from the premises to the conclusion.

Conclusion

The final claim you are trying to prove to the court.

Classifying Arguments

Type A

Deductive Arguments

Claims to provide conclusive grounds for its conclusion. It is an "All-or-Nothing" argument.

  • Evaluated by: Validity/Invalidity
  • Relationship: Strict necessity.
Type B

Inductive Arguments

Claims only a degree of probability. This is how most criminal investigations work.

  • Evaluated by: Strength/Weakness
  • Relationship: Probability (Higher or Lower).
Legal Special

Analogical Reasoning

Reasoning by Precedent. "If Case A was decided this way, and Case B is similar to Case A, then Case B should be decided the same way."

How to Spot an Argument (Indicator Words)

Law students must be "detectives" of language. Look for these keywords in legal texts:

Conclusion Indicators Premise Indicators
Therefore, Hence, Thus, So, Accordingly, In consequence, We may infer that... Because, Since, For, Given that, As indicated by, On the grounds that...

Critical Warning: Non-Arguments

Not all blocks of text are arguments. Beware of:

  • Explanations: Telling why something happened, not proving that it happened.
  • Conditional Statements: "If P, then Q" is a single proposition, not a full argument.
  • Reports: Simply stating facts without a concluding claim.
09. Conceptual Analysis

Logic of Concepts

A concept is a "Term" in its logical form. In Law, the battle is often fought over the boundaries of a single concept (e.g., Does the concept of "Vehicle" include a motorized wheelchair?).

Comprehension (Intension)

This refers to the internal content or qualities that make up the concept. It is the "Definition."

Example: The concept "Judge" includes: Human, Legal Expert, Presiding Officer.

Extension

This refers to the set of all objects that the concept applies to. It is the "Coverage."

Example: The extension of "Judge" includes every individual judge currently sitting in IMSU Moot Court or the Supreme Court.

The Law of Inverse Variation

As the Comprehension (details) of a concept increases, its Extension (number of things it covers) decreases.

More Detail ‚Üí Fewer Objects

Simple vs. Complex

Simple concepts (e.g., "Being") cannot be broken down. Complex concepts (e.g., "Contract of Sale") are made of many simpler parts.

Concrete vs. Abstract

Concrete concepts refer to physical objects (e.g., "Gavel"). Abstract concepts refer to qualities or relations (e.g., "Justice", "Equity").

Collective vs. Divisive

Collective refers to a group as a whole (e.g., "The Jury"). Divisive refers to each member individually.

09. Conceptual Perfection

The Quality of Concepts

In legal drafting, a concept must be "perfect" to avoid loopholes. We categorize them based on how clearly they represent their object and how they relate to others.

1. Clarity

Recognition

Clear: You can distinguish the object from everything else (e.g., "A Gavel").

Obscure: You cannot distinguish the object clearly; it is vague (e.g., "Justice" without a legal framework).

2. Adequacy

Definition

Adequate: You know all the essential features/notes of the concept (The full legal definition).

Inadequate: You recognize the object but can't list its defining characteristics (Knowing a "Crime" when you see it but not being able to define its elements).

Repugnant Concepts

Two concepts are Repugnant when they are mutually exclusive and cannot be applied to the same thing at the same time. This is a critical check for consistency in law.

10. The Syllogistic Formula

Terms in a Syllogism

A standard categorical syllogism contains exactly three terms, each appearing twice across the three propositions. Identifying them is the first step in checking for validity.

The Trio of Logic

1. The Major Term (P)

The predicate of the conclusion. It represents the broader category.

Location: Major Premise & Conclusion.

2. The Minor Term (S)

The subject of the conclusion. It represents the specific case or individual.

Location: Minor Premise & Conclusion.

3. The Middle Term (M)

The "Bridge." It connects the Major and Minor terms but never appears in the conclusion.

Location: Major Premise & Minor Premise.

The Logical Architecture

Major Premise: All [Lawyers] are [Educated].

Minor Premise: [Browne] is a [Lawyer].

Conclusion: Therefore, [Browne] is [Educated].

13. The Four-Fold Scheme

Standard Form Categorical Propositions

Every categorical proposition has a specific Letter Name (A, E, I, O). This system was developed to make the logic of language as precise as mathematics.

Type A

Universal Affirmative

Form: All S is P.

It includes the entire subject class within the predicate class.

"All Judges are Lawyers."

Type E

Universal Negative

Form: No S is P.

It excludes the entire subject class from the predicate class.

"No Felons are Voters."

Type I

Particular Affirmative

Form: Some S is P.

It asserts that at least one member of S is also a member of P.

"Some Laws are Unjust."

Type O

Particular Negative

Form: Some S is not P.

It asserts that at least one member of S is excluded from P.

"Some Crimes are not Felonies."

Visualizing the Classes

In 100lvl Logic, we use Venn Diagrams to visualize these relationships. An empty area means "Nothing exists here," while an 'X' means "At least one thing exists here."

14. Conditional Logic

Hypothetical Propositions

Unlike Categorical propositions, Hypothetical ones do not state facts directly. They state a relationship of dependence between two statements.

1. Conditional (If... Then)

Connects an Antecedent to a Consequent.

"If it rains (P), then the court is adjourned (Q)."

2. Disjunctive (Either... Or)

Presents two alternatives (members) that cannot both be false.

"Either the witness is lying, or the defendant is guilty."

15. Linguistic Translation

Standard vs. Non-Standard

Logic requires a strict format. Most sentences we speak are Non-Standard. To analyze them, we must translate them into the Standard A, E, I, O forms.

Non-Standard Form

Everyday language that is often vague, missing a quantifier, or uses non-standard verbs.

  • "Lawyers are rich."
  • "Not everyone is a thief."
  • "Birds fly."

Standard Form

Must have: Quantifier + Subject + Copula + Predicate.

  • "All lawyers are rich people." (A)
  • "Some people are not thieves." (O)
  • "All birds are flying creatures." (A)

Rules of Translation

1. Identify the Quantifier: Is it 'All', 'No', or 'Some'?

2. Fix the Copula: Always use 'is', 'are', 'is not', or 'are not'.

3. Standardize the Terms: The subject and predicate must be nouns or noun phrases.
